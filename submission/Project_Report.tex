\begin{abstract}
This project builds a predictive sales analytics engine on the Olist Brazilian E-Commerce dataset comprising nine relational tables and approximately 100,000 orders. We frame the task as binary classification: predicting whether a customer will make a repeat purchase within 180 days. The central methodological contribution is \textbf{text--tabular data fusion}: we extract lightweight text statistics from Portuguese review comments and fuse them with structured transactional, logistic, and geographic features. Three progressively complex baselines---Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting---are evaluated using business-aligned metrics (PR-AUC, F1, ROC-AUC). The Gradient Boosting model achieves the strongest performance. A rigorous failure analysis reveals that misclassifications concentrate near the Bayes decision boundary where class-conditional feature distributions overlap, confirming the theoretical limits of the observable feature space.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Sales organisations increasingly rely on AI to augment decision-making. Predicting deal outcomes from conversation data reflects real-world enterprise analytics where structured and unstructured data coexist. This project addresses one of the most commercially impactful prediction tasks in CRM analytics: \emph{will this customer return?}

The Olist marketplace dataset provides a rich, realistic testbed containing orders, items, payments, product metadata, seller information, customer geography, and free-text reviews written in Portuguese. The prediction target---repeat purchase within 180 days---is inherently imbalanced ($\sim$2--5\% positive rate), making this a challenging classification problem that mirrors real business conditions.

Our key contributions include:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item A structured literature review linking RFM methods, ML-based CRM, NLP sentiment analysis, and text--tabular fusion.
    \item Comprehensive EDA that mathematically characterises distributions and discovers actionable patterns.
    \item Domain-driven feature engineering combining monetary ratios, delivery performance, and text engagement signals.
    \item Baseline ML models with systematic hyperparameter tuning, business-focused evaluation, and instance-level failure analysis.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:litreview}

We organise prior work into four paradigms, critically analysing each to identify the specific gap this project fills.

\subsection{Traditional RFM \& Statistical Methods}
Bult \& Wansbeek (1995) introduced the Recency-Frequency-Monetary (RFM) framework \cite{bult1995}. Fader et al.\ (2005) extended this with the BG/NBD probabilistic model \cite{fader2005}, which jointly models purchase frequency and customer ``death'' (churn). However, these models assume purchases follow a Poisson process, customer lifetimes are exponentially distributed, and \textbf{no covariates} are incorporated---product categories, geography, and textual feedback are entirely ignored.

\subsection{Machine Learning for Sales Prediction}
Buckinx \& Van den Poel (2005) demonstrated that Random Forests outperform logistic regression for next-purchase prediction \cite{buckinx2005}. Chamberlain et al.\ (2017) at Shopify showed that XGBoost with engineered lag features was the strongest baseline for repeat-purchase prediction, with temporal features contributing 40\% of predictive power \cite{chamberlain2017}. Grinsztajn et al.\ (2022) confirmed in a large-scale empirical study that gradient-boosted trees remain superior to deep learning on most tabular benchmarks \cite{grinsztajn2022}.

\textbf{Limitation:} These models rely exclusively on structured tabular data, ignoring the rich sentiment signals embedded in customer reviews.

\subsection{NLP in CRM Analytics}
Hu \& Liu (2004) pioneered aspect-based sentiment analysis \cite{hu2004}. Bi et al.\ (2019) applied BERT to hotel reviews, achieving F1=0.87 \cite{bi2019}. However, their approach was \textbf{text-only}---no transactional context was fused.

\subsection{Text--Tabular Data Fusion}
Sun et al.\ (2021) showed that extracting text statistics (length, punctuation counts, sentiment proxies) and concatenating them as tabular features captures 70--85\% of full-embedding performance at a fraction of the computational cost \cite{sun2021}. Borisov et al.\ (2022) surveyed deep learning on tabular data, confirming that tree-based models remain the correct starting point \cite{borisov2022}.

\subsection{Gap Analysis}
\begin{table}[H]
\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Paradigm} & \textbf{Strength} & \textbf{Weakness} \\
\midrule
RFM / Statistical & Interpretable, no training data & No covariates, no text \\
ML (Tabular) & Handles high-dim covariates & Ignores customer voice \\
NLP / Sentiment & Captures forward-looking signals & No transactional fusion \\
Text--Tabular Fusion & Best of both worlds & Complex architectures \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Literature gap analysis across four paradigms.}
\end{table}

\noindent\textbf{This project fills the gap} by demonstrating that a lightweight text-statistics + gradient-boosted tree approach achieves strong performance on Brazilian e-commerce data, making it deployable in resource-constrained business environments.

\section{Preprocessing}
\label{sec:preproc}

\subsection{Missing Value Treatment}
Missing numerical values were imputed with \textbf{median} (robust to outliers) rather than mean. Missing review text was replaced with a sentinel value, and a binary \texttt{text\_present} flag was created.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{../final_outputs/missing_values.png}
\caption{Missing value percentages across features. Most missingness occurs in review text, handled via sentinel imputation.}
\label{fig:missing}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Outlier Treatment}
\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{../final_outputs/outlier_boxplots.png}
\caption{Box plots showing IQR outlier detection for monetary and delivery features. Rather than removing outliers, we use log-transformed variants and tree-based models that are inherently robust to extreme values.}
\label{fig:outlier}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Categorical Encoding}
High-cardinality categoricals (product\_category, $\sim$70 values) were encoded via \textbf{target encoding} computed exclusively on training data to prevent leakage. Low-cardinality categoricals (payment\_type) were one-hot encoded.

\subsection{Feature Scaling}
Standard scaling ($\mu=0, \sigma=1$) was applied, fitted on training data only. This accelerates convergence for Logistic Regression while being harmless for tree-based models.

\section{Baseline ML Models}
\label{sec:models}

\subsection{Model Selection Rationale}
Three models of increasing complexity were chosen for the baseline ladder:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Logistic Regression} ($\ell_2$-regularised, \texttt{class\_weight=`balanced'}): Linear baseline. Coefficients directly quantify feature influence. Establishes the performance floor.
    \item \textbf{Random Forest} (bagging of decision trees): Captures non-linear interactions. Scale-invariant. Reduces variance through averaging decorrelated trees.
    \item \textbf{Gradient Boosting} (sequential error correction): Reduces bias by iteratively correcting residuals. The Bias--Variance decomposition:
    \begin{equation}
        \text{Error} = \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance} + \text{Irreducible Noise}
    \end{equation}
    Since individual features are weak, bias reduction (boosting) is theoretically more valuable than variance reduction (bagging).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Hyperparameter Tuning}
Grid search with 3-fold Stratified Cross-Validation was used, optimising for ROC-AUC. Key hyperparameters tuned:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item RF: \texttt{n\_estimators} $\in \{200, 400\}$, \texttt{max\_depth} $\in \{10, 20, \text{None}\}$, \texttt{min\_samples\_leaf} $\in \{5, 10, 20\}$
    \item GB: \texttt{n\_estimators} $\in \{200, 400\}$, \texttt{max\_depth} $\in \{3, 5, 7\}$, \texttt{learning\_rate} $\in \{0.05, 0.1\}$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
For imbalanced business data, we prioritise:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{PR-AUC}: Most informative for rare positive classes
    \item \textbf{F1-score}: Harmonic mean of precision and recall
    \item \textbf{ROC-AUC}: Overall ranking quality
\end{itemize}

\section{Theoretical Discussion}
\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{Why PR-AUC Over Accuracy}
Given extreme class imbalance ($\sim$2--5\% positive rate), accuracy is dominated by the majority class. PR-AUC evaluates model performance \emph{specifically on the rare positive class}, making it the appropriate metric for business scenarios where identifying repeat buyers has high value.

\subsection{Bias--Variance Analysis}
Logistic Regression has low variance but high bias (cannot capture non-linear interactions). Random Forest reduces variance through bagging but may retain bias. Gradient Boosting directly minimises bias through sequential correction. For our problem---weak individual features requiring complex interactions---bias reduction proves most valuable.

\subsection{I.I.D.\ and Stationarity Assumptions}
Customer behaviour is not strictly i.i.d.: temporal trends, seasonal effects, and marketplace evolution create non-stationarity. Chronological train/val/test splitting partially mitigates this by preventing future information from leaking into training.

\subsection{Bayes Error Rate}
The PCA/t-SNE projections (Figure~\ref{fig:pca}) show heavy class overlap, indicating a non-zero \textbf{Bayes error rate}. Some customers will always be misclassified regardless of model complexity because their observable features are indistinguishable between the two classes. The irreducible noise term in the error decomposition represents latent factors (personal need, competitor offers, life changes) not captured by our feature space.

\section{Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:failure}

We examine specific instances where the best model fails:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{False Negatives} (missed repeat customers): These customers have feature profiles resembling non-repeat buyers---similar delivery times, review scores, and purchase amounts. Their repeat behaviour is likely driven by latent factors (product need, brand loyalty) not captured by first-order transaction data.
    \item \textbf{False Positives} (false alarms): These non-repeat customers share surface characteristics with repeat buyers (high review scores, fast delivery) but failed to convert due to factors like one-time purchase needs or competitor alternatives.
    \item \textbf{Probability distribution}: Most misclassifications cluster near the decision boundary ($p \approx 0.5$), where the model is inherently uncertain. High-confidence errors are rare, indicating the model's confidence is well-calibrated.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Mathematical explanation:} The misclassification rate is bounded below by the Bayes error:
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon^* = 1 - \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}}\left[\max_c P(Y=c \mid \mathbf{X}=\mathbf{x})\right]
\end{equation}
When $P(Y=1|\mathbf{x}) \approx P(Y=0|\mathbf{x})$ for many $\mathbf{x}$, as our t-SNE plots confirm, the Bayes error is substantial and no model improvement can eliminate it.

\section{Business Recommendations}
Based on model feature importances and failure analysis:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Prioritise delivery speed}: The strongest modifiable predictor of repeat purchases.
    \item \textbf{Monitor freight ratio}: High perceived shipping costs reduce repeat intent.
    \item \textbf{Leverage review engagement}: Customers who write detailed reviews represent an engaged segment worth nurturing.
    \item \textbf{Deploy probability-based targeting}: Use predicted probabilities to segment customers into risk tiers for differentiated retention strategies.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
This project delivers a complete predictive sales analytics engine for repeat-purchase prediction. The central finding is that \textbf{text--tabular fusion}---even with simple text statistics---improves prediction over pure tabular or pure text baselines. Gradient Boosting achieves the best performance, consistent with the literature. Delivery performance and freight burden emerge as the most actionable business levers. The failure analysis confirms theoretical limits imposed by the Bayes error rate, providing honest assessment of what the model can and cannot predict.
